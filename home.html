GarageBand Analysis

Introduction

Most electronic music softwares have an extremely steep learning curve, making it difficult for new producers to gain experience producing. GarageBand is geared towards beginners with its use of extensive libraries, graphical descriptions, and simplified editing tools, catching new producers up to speed before jumping into more studio-level software.


User Research

Interview Questions

Background

  • Have you ever used a music producing software before today?
  • Talk to me about your musical background, whether it is electronic or not. How long have you been involved with music, or have had any interest in music, or listened to music, and what kind of experiences do you have in those respects?
  • (for those who have used it before) What have you used GarageBand for?

Lightly-Guided Navigation

  • On a scale of 1 to 10, with 1 being easiest and 10 being most difficult, how would you rate the navigation for instruments?
  • On a scale of 1 to 10, with 1 being easiest and 10 being most difficult, how would you rate the means of adding instrument tracks into the project?
  • Could you please input some notes into a track for me?
  • Could you try to make changes to some of the notes you already recorded?
  • Can you try to add a looping music segment onto the track?
  • Can you change the key and tempo of the project?
  • Clarifying automation, it is a technique in electronic music production where you essentially program the computer to make changes to your track as you play music. On most music softwares, this is a graphical process, so there's no code or anything like that involved. With this knowledge, can you try to find automation settings and add some on this track?

Reflecting

  • What is one part of this interface that made your life easier or harder? Talk about that with me.
  • On a scale of 1 to 10, how would you rate your comfort with this software?
  • (for those who indicated less music experience) How would you rate your ability to adapt to this interface from 1 to 10, 1 being no way and 10 being it feels natural? In other words, if I asked you to create a song using this, how comfortable or uncomfortable would you feel with that task?
  • (for those who indicated GarageBand experience) How would you feel this software has or may have prepared you for more advanced music production software?
  • What is something you felt was unclear as you were navigating this interface, and why?
  • What is something all beginner producers should know that may or may not be accessible on GarageBand?
  • On a scale of 1 to 10, with 1 being no way and 10 being with ease, how would you rate your ability to develop a fully-fleshed out vision of a project on Garageband?

Responses

Anonymous User 1

Summary: User A is inexperienced with both music and music production. They made extensive use of the quick help, which they praised for pointing directly to anything being hovered over. They tended to search more for buttons to navigate libraries rather than scroll bars. They had a hard time finding the display and means to change tempo and key. Metronome controls were simple because there were moving parts on the button. Opening tracks was very intuitive to them but things requiring clicking were not.

MAIN TAKEAWAY: Buttons are very beginner-friendly, especially if labelled with clear icons.

Anonymous User 2

Summary: User B is inexperienced with digital music but heavily knowledgeable with music creation and performance. They showed heavy frustration with navigating menus, particularly those not on the interface itself but on the top bar menu - these were seen as less important to the function of the interface. They had difficulty locating key and tempo information. They managed to do well despite not using the quick help as much.

MAIN TAKEAWAY: Functions relegated to the top bar of the interface appear to be more administrative tasks, and thus less relevant to what is on the interface.

Anonymous User 3

Summary: User C is heavily experienced with both music performance and production. Navigating the various libraries was relatively simple for them. However, they had initially struggled with the interface for recording notes and being able to find musical typing. They felt that the interface was captivating enough to want to stay on it “for hours on end, until very late at night.”

MAIN TAKEAWAY: Crucial tools, such as musical typing, should be displayed more prominently on the interface.

Personas

Personas are essential in the realm of software development because they allow designers to empathize with the users of their end product by creating people and crafting their stories, and thinking about designs that would leave these personas satisfied. For GarageBand, I crafted two distinct personas.


Sage West (22, she/they)

Sage was going through their computer when they found this app pre-installed on their laptop. Despite having minimal experience with music or music production, this app looked kinda cool so she decided to mess around and see what she could make.


This persona represents a flighty intellectually-curious user, who stumbles upon Garageband and starts using it due to sheer curiosity. The persona exists as a stand-in for a user who has very minimal experience with music as a whole but is pushed into their passion via this resource. They will struggle with the various menus in the app and will be easily frustrated by tasks deemed too difficult.

Thinks
  • There’s a lot of cool instruments on here, let’s see if I can get a guitar sound
  • How do you play sounds on this thing? How do I add sounds?
  • What the heck did I press and where did that box go?
  • Why are there so many extra windows?
Says
  • “I found this thing on my computer and it looks kinda cool”
  • “I didn’t really wanna do my assignments so I was just fiddling with things.”
  • “These loop things are so easy to use, I might just mess with these instead of bothering with the whole instrument thing”
Does
  • Turns off the quick help because too many pop-up dialogues
  • Primarily uses loops since it’s the easiest to work with
  • After figuring out musical typing, switching through all the instruments
Feels
  • Confused at how the whole thing works
  • Intrigued because fun pictures
  • Exhausted because using this to procrastinate on work

Martin Wallace-Johnson (43, he/him)

Martin has been writing sheet music for ages, but has decided he wants to “get with the kids” as they say and try out electronic production. He’s looking for good ways to translate his analog music experience into a digital music ability.


This persona represents users who want to take their already-existing music experience a step further. This particular user is not the most technologically-savvy, and will take time out of their day to work through the software so it doesn’t interrupt flows of ideas when actually working on pieces.

Thinks
  • I can’t wrap my brain around all of these commands
  • There are so many ideas in my head right now that I’m having a hard time putting onto this screen
  • Where am I supposed to click now?
Says
  • “Music just comes naturally to me, I’ll pick this up so easily”
  • “Got an idea for a piece so I won’t be coming with you all tonight”
  • “If I could spend all day creating music, I absolutely would”
Does
  • Sings to keep ideas in his head
  • Spends hours navigating through menus to understand the ins-and-outs before making anything
  • Attempts to play all the notes accurately on musical typing
  • Primarily uses musical typing and recording
Feels
  • Excited at the score recreation of MIDI inputs
  • Confusion at how to manipulate notes in that layout and at how to add dynamic changes
  • Invested in getting it all just right
